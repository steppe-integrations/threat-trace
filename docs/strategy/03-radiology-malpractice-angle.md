# 03 — Radiology malpractice-carrier angle

## Purpose

In radiology, the practitioner (radiologist) and the budget-holder (rad group / hospital) are the obvious buyers — but selling SaaS into rad groups is hard, slow, and competitive. **The malpractice carrier may be the actual leverage point**: if a carrier discounts premiums for documented AI competency, the carrier becomes the buyer (or co-buyer), the rad group becomes the user, and the GTM motion completely changes. This brief tests whether that dynamic is viable.

## Key questions

- Do any major rad-mal carriers (The Doctors Company, NORCAL/ProAssurance, MagMutual, Coverys) currently offer **premium adjustments** for documented AI competency or oversight?
- Is there an existing "AI competency" certification (ACR, RSNA, others) that carriers recognize?
- How are carriers currently underwriting AI-related radiology risk — at all, or as part of standard practice?
- Is this a **B2B2C** play (sell to carrier, carrier provides to insureds as a benefit) or a **B2B with carrier blessing** (sell to rad groups, carrier endorses)?
- Are the brokers more accessible than carriers, and is that the right entry point?

## Who to talk to

- 1 underwriter at a major rad-mal carrier (hardest to reach; try via broker)
- 1–2 brokers who place rad-mal coverage (more accessible than carriers)
- 1 RCRMP-credentialed risk manager at a hospital system
- 1 ACR (American College of Radiology) program contact — they may have an AI certification track or be planning one
- 1 rep at a healthcare AI vendor with mammography/stroke deployments (Aidoc, Lunit, iCAD) — what do their customers say about insurance?
- 1 academic in radiology AI adoption (Mass General Brigham AI center, Stanford AIMI)

## Talking prompts

- **To underwriter / broker**: "Are you currently asking insureds about their AI use in your underwriting questionnaires? Do you offer any premium adjustment for documented training, oversight, or governance?"
- **To broker**: "If a tool existed that produced a competency artifact for every AI-assisted read — radiologist + AI agreement, override decisions, override rationale — would carriers care? Would they discount?"
- **To rad group risk manager**: "What would your malpractice carrier need to see to feel comfortable with you using AI tools in mammography or stroke triage?"
- **To ACR**: "Do you have or plan an AI competency designation? Who decides what counts?"
- **To AI vendor**: "When your customers deploy your tool, what insurance / liability conversations come up? Have any carriers reached out?"

## Decision tree

| Carrier reality today | Implication |
|---|---|
| Carriers already discount for AI competency | Fast wedge — partner with broker, target carrier-blessed offering. Highest leverage path. |
| Carriers are *thinking about* it but not doing it yet | 6–12 month design partnership opportunity with one carrier. High leverage, slower. |
| Carriers don't see AI as relevant to underwriting | Revert to direct-to-rad-group sale — much harder, lower margin. Reconsider radiology vs other verticals. |
| ACR or another body already runs an AI cert program | Partner / license rather than compete. Fast path to credibility. |

## Starter steps (this week)

1. Pull 3–5 rad-mal underwriting questionnaires (most carriers publish these or they're in broker submissions). Note any AI-related questions.
2. Find AHRMNY / ASHRM / RIMS proceedings on radiology AI risk — read the latest 2 conference summaries.
3. Reach out to 1 broker via LinkedIn (insurance brokers are often surprisingly responsive to founder cold outreach if the topic is novel).
4. Sketch what a "radiologist competency artifact" output would look like — 1-page mock that a carrier could review.
5. Email 1 ACR program contact (publicly listed on acr.org) about AI competency programs.

## Risks / caveats

- **Insurance is slow.** Even a YES from a carrier takes 12+ months to translate into a productized program. Plan dual-track GTM (carrier + direct).
- The rad-mal market is **concentrated** — if the top 3 carriers all say no, the carrier path closes. Validate breadth before committing.
- Rad groups may **resent insurance-mandated training** as an autonomy-violation. Cultural friction is real; positioning matters.
- ACR or a similar body could **own this themselves** — if so, the play becomes white-labeling to them, not direct sale.
- Adoption may be blocked by **EHR / PACS integration**, not by training. Verify the rehearsal artifact is the actual gating issue, not a downstream nice-to-have.
- Even with a discount, the user (radiologist) still has to *want* to use the tool. The carrier sale solves distribution, not adoption.
