# 04 — Anti-thesis interviews

## Purpose

The AI explainability / interpretability / model-monitoring space has a startup graveyard from 2018–2022 — Truera, parts of Fiddler, Arthur, etc. Many raised, few broke out. Before betting that "AI trust + agent rehearsal" is suddenly a viable category in 2026, we need to **diligence what killed the prior cycle** and pressure-test whether the things that are different now are *really* different.

## Key questions

- Why did the 2018–2022 explainability / interpretability startup wave fail to produce a breakout standalone category?
- What concrete buyer feedback killed those bets — pricing, urgency, packaging, persona, value prop?
- What's structurally different in 2026 — different buyer, different urgency, different product shape, different distribution, different regulatory pressure?
- Is "AI trust / transparency" a category that **consistently** underperforms expectations, or are we in a genuinely new cycle driven by agent autonomy + regulatory tailwinds?
- What did the survivors (Arize, Fiddler, Arthur, Credo AI) have to do to keep going? What did they *give up* to survive?

## Who to talk to

- **2 ex-founders** of 2019–2022 explainability/interpretability startups that pivoted, were acquihired, or shut down (Truera was acquired by Snowflake — talk to ex-Truera people)
- **1 founder** still operating in the space (Fiddler, Arthur, Arize, Credo AI, Holistic AI)
- **1 VC** who invested in the prior cycle and is now skeptical (or one who's bullish on agent governance specifically)
- **1 enterprise AI buyer** who *evaluated* prior-cycle interpretability tools and chose not to buy — what was missing?
- **1 industry analyst** (Gartner / Forrester) who covered the space then and now

## Talking prompts

- **To ex-founder**: "When buyers passed on you, what was the *real* reason — was it priority, budget, fundamental disinterest in the value prop, or 'we'll get this from our platform vendor'?"
- **To still-running founder**: "What's your wedge against incumbents (cloud hyperscalers, model labs) building this in? What did you have to give up to survive?"
- **To VC**: "Is 'AI trust / transparency' a category that always ends up as a feature inside the platform, or do you see 2026 as structurally different?"
- **To enterprise buyer**: "When you looked at [Truera / Fiddler / Arthur], what was missing that would have made you buy? What did you end up using instead?"
- **Sharpest question**: "If you were starting that company today in the agent era, what would you do differently?"

## Decision tree

| Lesson learned | Implication for this bet |
|---|---|
| "Buyers wanted it bundled with their platform" | Reinforces 01 finding if it lands the same way. Pivot weight to OSS (06) and audit (10). |
| "The buyer wasn't urgent enough" | The 2026 regulatory tailwinds (FinCEN, NAIC, EU AI Act) *could* change this — but that needs validation. Without urgency, the bet fails the same way. |
| "The product shape was wrong (post-hoc dashboards, not active workflow)" | threat-trace's *pre-deployment, workflow-rehearsal* angle is the structural differentiator. Lean into it. |
| "The right buyer never got identified" | Elevates briefs 02 / 03 — buyer-channel research is even more important. |
| "Distribution was the problem, not the product" | Reinforces partnership map (07) and acquirer thesis. Strongly favors going through Big 4 / vendors / carriers, not direct sale. |

## Starter steps (this week)

1. LinkedIn search ex-Truera, ex-Fiddler, ex-Arthur employees who moved on. Filter for founders, heads of product, heads of GTM.
2. Read post-mortems / "what I learned" Substack/Medium posts (there are several from 2023–2024).
3. Reach out to 2 ex-founders. Offer reciprocity (a 30-min call where you share your own thesis in exchange).
4. Read 1 Gartner or Forrester report from 2021 and one from 2026 on this space. Diff them.
5. Document patterns in a 1-page "what killed the prior cycle, what's different now" memo.

## Risks / caveats

- Founders are **reluctant to share negative lessons publicly** — may need warm intro or an explicit "this is anonymous research." Don't expect candor on first contact.
- **Survivor bias** — companies still alive will tell you "we were wrong about X but right about Y." That narrative is curated; treat it as a hypothesis to verify, not a finding.
- **Failed founders may not respond at all.** Cast a wide net; 1 useful response per 5 outreaches is good.
- Even if the prior cycle's lessons are clear, **2026 conditions may genuinely differ enough** that they don't apply. Don't over-index on history; weight it ~50%.
- This brief is high-information-value but **not on the critical path**. Don't let it block executing on briefs 01, 02, 09. Run in parallel.
