# 07 — Partnership / acquisition map

## Purpose

Knowing **who would buy this in 12–24 months** shapes what you build now. Knowing **who would partner now** shapes how you distribute. This brief maps both, with the explicit warning: building for an acquirer corrupts product decisions. Use this as situational awareness, not as a North Star.

## Key questions

- Who is the most likely acquirer profile — AI agent vendor, security platform, foundation model lab, training/cert provider, Big 4?
- What strategic pain does each acquirer have that this product would relieve?
- Who has distribution into our target buyers but doesn't compete (partner, not buyer)?
- What's the defensible reference architecture / data asset / customer base they couldn't easily build themselves in 6 months?
- What does the **Cyberbit / RangeForce** acquisition imply about pricing and multiple in this category?

## Acquirer hypotheses (rank, then validate)

| Acquirer | Strategic gap we close | Likelihood | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cyberbit** (just bought RangeForce) | Pivoting from human skill training to AI familiarization is natural; RangeForce + threat-trace = full-stack security training | High | Most aligned acquirer; recent deal sets a multiple |
| **Prophet Security / Dropzone AI** | Bolt teaching layer onto existing agent product; pre-sales enablement | Medium-High | Only if we have meaningful customer base and aren't a feature gap they fix in-house first |
| **Big 4 (Deloitte / PwC / EY / KPMG)** | AI assurance practice tooling — overlaps with audit play (brief 10) | Medium | Would acquire for the framework + content, especially if audit play matures |
| **SANS / ACAMS / ACR / professional bodies** | License or acquire as a certification track | Low-Medium | Specialized exit, lower multiple, highly aligned mission |
| **Anthropic / OpenAI** | Customer-trust / enablement tooling for own agent platforms | Low | Would tie product to their platform; deprioritizes vertical breadth |
| **Splunk / Microsoft Sentinel / Palo Alto** | Security platform broadening into AI governance | Medium | Long sales cycles inside; likely wait for a category leader to emerge |
| **Insurance carriers (cyber / E&O / rad-mal)** | Risk-reduction product to bundle with policies | Low-Medium | Partnership more likely than acquisition; see brief 03 |

## Partner hypotheses (start now, low cost)

These are co-distribution conversations, not deals.

- **AI agent vendors who want a "trial / familiarization" experience** for their buyers (Prophet, Dropzone, Torq, Innovaccer, Unit21, Lucinity) — pre-sales motion. We make them look better; they give us references.
- **Insurance brokers** (rad-mal, cyber, E&O) — co-distribution into regulated buyers. See brief 03.
- **Industry associations** (ACR, ACAMS, SANS, ISACA, IIA) — co-curriculum, co-branding, certification track. Long-cycle but high-credibility.
- **System integrators** (Optiv, Mandiant, Accenture Federal) — co-deliver into enterprise. Best for AML and federal.
- **Law firms / Big 4 advisory** — sell into AI governance committees as part of larger engagements. Aligned with audit play.

## Decision tree

| If clearest acquirer is… | Build implication |
|---|---|
| **Cyberbit** | Build security + adjacent (insurance fraud, AML) verticals so it looks like a portfolio, not a one-trick demo |
| **Big 4** | Lean into audit play (brief 10), AI assurance positioning, framework + content separation |
| **Agent vendors (Prophet/Dropzone)** | Build vendor-neutrality and multi-vertical proof — the thing they *can't* easily build |
| **Unclear** | Focus on partner-led distribution, defer the exit thesis. Don't optimize for an acquirer that may not exist in 18 months. |

## Starter steps (this week)

1. **Make a 1-pager per top-3 acquirers**: their stated strategy, their gaps, what we'd bring, what evidence would change their mind. Keep it tight; this is a thinking artifact, not a pitch.
2. Identify **2–3 partner conversations** to start *now* — informational, not deal-making. Top picks: 1 agent vendor (Prophet or Dropzone), 1 industry association (ACR or ACAMS), 1 system integrator.
3. Track recent **M&A activity** in the space — Cyberbit/RangeForce sets a comparable. Watch for next 1–2 deals to confirm or revise the multiple.
4. Decide one principle: **are we building for an exit or for a long-term company?** The answer changes everything else. (You don't have to lock it; do write it down.)
5. Set a calendar reminder for a 6-month re-evaluation — acquirer landscape shifts faster than product strategy.

## Risks / caveats

- **Building for an acquirer corrupts product decisions.** Build for users. Exit shapes itself if the product is real. This brief is awareness, not a target.
- **Acquirer landscape changes fast.** The right 2026 buyer may be irrelevant by 2027. Don't over-commit to one path.
- **Partnership conversations can leak the idea.** Be careful what you share, especially with potential acquirers who could decide to build instead of buy.
- Conversations with potential acquirers double as **competitive intelligence for them**. If they're 6 months away from building this in-house, your meeting may accelerate it.
- **Bundling vs feature risk** (from brief 01) re-appears here: if the answer to 01 is "buyers want it bundled," many of these acquirers become competitors instead of buyers.
